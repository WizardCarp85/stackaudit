# User Interviews

This document contains notes and feedback from user interviews regarding StackAudit.

## Interview 1: DS
**Profile:** 2nd-year B.Design student at Rishihood University.  
**Company Stage:** N/A (Student User)

**Key Quotes:**
> "I need a bit more info about what the website actually does right when I land on it."
> "You should really add pricing for INR too, to make it easier for Indian people to understand the cost."
> "There should definitely be an option for an instant Credex redirect just below the AI summary."

**Most Surprising Thing:**
* Despite approaching the app from a pure design perspective, their most valuable feedback was highly functional. They were surprisingly focused on how visual cues could drive user action—specifically noting that placing logos in front of the AI models' names didn't just make the UI look better, but instinctively signaled that the logo should be a clickable link to redirect out to that specific tool's pricing page.

**What it changed about your design:**
* It shifted my future focus toward clearer onboarding. In the next iteration, I plan to add a much more prominent explanation of the tool's utility right at the top of the page. Moving forward, I will also implement an INR pricing toggle, and I intend to reposition the Credex redirect button directly beneath the AI summary to capitalize on that user intent.

## Interview 2: CosmicMagnetar
**Profile:** Founder of OpenTriage (AI-powered developer dashboard); 2nd-year B.Tech CS-AI student at NST.  
**Company Stage:** Active Early-Stage Development.

**Key Quotes:**
> "Honestly, this needs to be a mobile app. Managing these services is the kind of 'life admin' task most people want to handle on their phone during a break, not while sitting at a desk."
> "The UI has to be dark mode by default. Most developers and AI power users spend their entire day in dark environments; a bright white screen feels like an interruption to the workflow."
> "I need to see the actual utility of what I'm buying. Show me a graph of my spending versus how much I actually use the tool so I can decide if it’s time to switch to a different service."

**Most Surprising Thing:**
* Despite being a power user who handles complex AI architectures, his main frustration wasn't about technical features—it was about "tool fatigue." He was surprisingly focused on the psychological side of managing services, specifically needing the app to tell him if a tool was still useful for his current workload. He doesn't just want a tracker; he wants a tool that tells him when to stop spending money.

**What it changed about your design:**
* It completely flipped my priority list for future theming. Because he pointed out how a light screen interrupts a developer's workflow, I will prioritize adding a dark mode by default in the upcoming updates. I will also try to design a new "utilization graph" layout that visually maps spending against usage, directly addressing his core problem of tool fatigue.

## Interview 3: KD
**Profile:** 2nd-year B.Tech student at NST.  
**Company Stage:** Founder of Urjasaarthi.

**Key Quotes:**
> "I still don’t know what exact problem you solve in the first 15 seconds."
> "The animations make it feel premium, but I need actionable outputs, not vibes."
> "If you claim audits, show me one brutal comparison instantly."

**Most Surprising Thing:**
* What surprised me the most was that he didn't actually care about the stack detection itself. He already knows tools like Vercel and Supabase exist. His real frustration lies in the uncertainty of decision-making—he specifically wants to know which stack becomes expensive later, which scales badly, and what the hidden tradeoffs are.

**What it changed about your design:**
* It will completely reshape the homepage to be more outcome-driven rather than abstract. Moving forward, I plan to significantly reduce the cinematic messaging and will immediately display a real, brutal audit comparison right at the top of the page. This will ensure the value delivery happens much faster and makes the platform feel genuinely trustworthy.
